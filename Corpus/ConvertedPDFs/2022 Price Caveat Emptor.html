THE PROBLEM
If you have found this challenging or have never seen the need to do so, read on.
H ave you ever tried to determine whether you can safely use a product in your dental ofﬁce?
For good safety reasons, most countries regulate many devices and products used in
dental ofﬁces1-3 using standards developed by organizations such as the International Organization
for Standardization (ISO) and the American National Standards Institute. An example of these
standards can be found in how the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) classiﬁes dental resin-
based composites as Class II medical devices.4 When dentists purchase unregulated devices, such as
personal protective equipment (PPE), dental drills, radiography equipment, or curing lights, patient
safety is jeopardized and reputations can be easily ruined.5-11
Dental manufacturers and supply companies are required to ensure that the products they sell
comply with all of the relevant health and safety regulations. However, considerations of cost and
convenience, and now COVID-19, have fueled the online purchasing of regulated medical
devices.5,9,10,12,13 In 2019, it was reported that as much as 11% of the dental supply market had
already shifted to online sources,12 and sellers can ﬁnd resources such as “Selling FDA Regulated
Products on Amazon: Tips, Tricks, and Warnings.”14 Despite warnings to beware of purchasing
cheap medical and dental devices,7,9-12,15 many such devices and products can now be purchased
from various online marketplaces. However, determining the veracity of the online claims16,17 made
for such products is problematic, and bad choices can affect your career.7,9,11 For example, in 2016, a
dentist in the United Kingdom appeared before the General Dental Council after purchasing and
using counterfeit products from an online site. Despite claiming that he was not aware that the
products were counterfeit, the committee deemed that this should have been clear from the
products’
low prices and found the general dental practitioner to be acting with ﬁnancial
motivation.9
Although the web-hosting company may state that products offered for sale “must comply with all
laws and regulations” and that “products that claim to be ‘FDA cleared,’
‘FDA approved,’ or
products that include the FDA logo in associated images need to meet additional requirements ...”18
in reality, the FDA has found that the ﬁlters used by the website are often inadequate.19 In addition,
the web-hosting company may try to take no responsibility for the products they sell. For example,
Paragraph 6.1 of the Amazon Services Business Solutions Agreement states that the seller “will
defend, indemnify, and hold harmless Amazon . against any third-party claim, loss, damage,
settlement, cost, expense, or other liability (including, without limitation, attorneys’ fees) . arising
from or related to (a) your non-compliance with applicable Laws; . .”20 As a consequence, a dental
curing light that carries both the FDA and Conformité Européenne (CE) logos can be purchased
from an online marketplace (Alibaba) for just $1.16 But how does the purchaser verify that the
product can legitimately carry both logos? Although some resources do exist, conﬁrming the ve-
racity of online marketplace claims using FDA or Health Canada websites21,22 that should contain
this information, is difﬁcult if not impossible.
This problem is not limited to dentistry. The World Health Organization has estimated that 1 in
10 medical products in low- and middle-income countries is substandard or falsiﬁed.23 In 2021,
Interpol reported that fake and unauthorized COVID-19 testing kits accounted for more than one-
half of all medical devices seized in Operation Pangea XIV.24 As a consequence, there is a
comprehensive global strategy to increase access to safe and effective medical products.23 As part of
Operation Pangea to tackle the online sale of illegal products and devices, in 2021, UK ofﬁcers
seized more than 3 million medicines and medical devices in just 1 week. They also removed or

A BRIEF REVIEW OF THE REGULATORY PROCESS IN THE UNITED STATES AND CANADA
In the United States, the FDA classiﬁes PPE and dental devices as medical devices in the Code of
Federal Regulations (CFR) Part 872.1 Before the products are sold in the United States, they must
be cleared or approved by the FDA under Section 510(k) of the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic
Act.26,27 Under this Act, there are civil and criminal penalties for selling nonapproved medical
devices in the United States, and the FDA has the authority for an injunction (Section 302), seizure
(Section 304), and referral for criminal prosecution (Section 305).1 It was reported that in just the
last week of April 2021, Cincinnati US Customs and Border Protection ofﬁcers and the FDA Ofﬁce
of Criminal Investigations seized hundreds of shipments containing prohibited and counterfeit
regulated drugs and devices.13 Because the FDA states that an unapproved medical device may be
used on human participants only when the device is under clinical investigation or when it is used
by investigators participating in a clinical trial,28 it is reasonable that US dentists should expect that
accurate and up-to-date information is readily available from the FDA to help them determine what
devices they can use in their dental ofﬁces. However, we have found that this is not an easy task.
As stated above, dental devices are classiﬁed as medical devices in 21 CFR Part 872.1 The FDA
uses the term “medical products” for drugs or combination drug-device products. The FDA uses a
risk-based, tiered control approach to determine whether a medical device is safe and effective.1,26,27
The FDA uses the term “approved” for devices that are subject to Premarket Approval (PMA)
(Class III devices). A PMA is given because the submission has been deemed to establish the safety
and effectiveness of the device. Unlike the 510(k) clearance process for Class II devices, PMAs are
not comparative. The FDA “clears” devices as equivalent to a predicate device based on the
documentation it receives in a Premarket Notiﬁcation [510(k)] submission claim,26,27 that is, in a
comparative evaluation with a device that has already been cleared for sale.26 However, sponsors are
not allowed to advertise their 510(k)-cleared products as “FDA cleared” or “FDA approved.” This is
considered misbranding under 21 CFR 807.97.1 The same applies to the use of the FDA logo on
devices. Indeed, the FDA is unequivocal in that its name should never be used to suggest that they
endorse or approve any product,29 and though Amazon warns its sellers about making such claims, it
still happens.18
Unless there is a reported problem, neither the FDA nor Health Canada tests products, but they
do review the sale of medical devices19 and will conduct inspections with possible regulatory
consequences. For example, in 2012, the FDA issued a safety communication regarding the sale of
potentially unsafe hand-held dental radiography units because they did not comply with FDA re-
quirements.6,8 The FDA has also sent warning letters to Amazon reminding them that it is their
responsibility to ensure that they comply with all requirements of federal law and FDA regula-
tions.19 However, controlling the sale of medical devices purchased online from an overseas
marketplace16 is problematic because purchases are shipped directly to customers.21 Problems may
then surface only through media stories or postings on the FDA MedWatch program.30 The FDA
also welcomes inquiries about questionable products at CDRHDeviceAllegations@fda.hhs.gov.
Other countries have similar sites.
The FDA does provide a guide on how to discover whether it has cleared a medical device,31 and
a database of safe or recalled medical devices can be found by searching the internet for “FDA-
approved devices.”22 Unfortunately, companies sometimes change the name of a product after it has
received clearance, making it challenging to locate a particular product under the new name. An
alternative database is available at the US National Library of Medicine website.32 This Global
Unique Device Identiﬁcation Database (GUDID) is based on many regulations worldwide,
particularly from the United States, the European Union, China, the Republic of Korea, and Saudi
Arabia. The GUDID uses the unique device identiﬁer (UDI) assigned to a product.33 However, this
database is still somewhat confusing to use, inaccurate, and the results are not easily exported for
comparison purposes.
In Canada, medical devices are classiﬁed into 1 of 4 classes. Class I represents the lowest risk and
Class IV the highest. Health Canada takes legal measures to protect consumers from illegal, false, or
misleading advertising of medical devices. Health Canada also provides advice on what to look for
when buying medical devices from the internet,21 but, unfortunately, dental curing lights are

REGULATORY PROCESS IN EUROPE
In the United Kingdom, the Medicines & Healthcare Products Regulatory Agency seizes tens of
thousands of counterfeit and noncompliant dental devices every year.9 The British Dental Industry
Association has launched a Counterfeit and Substandard Instruments and Devices Initiative11,36 to
promote awareness of the dangers of counterfeit and illegal dental instruments and devices. It also
has a directory of trustworthy dental products and services.37
In the European Union, Regulation (EU) 2017/745 for medical devices2 came into effect in May
2021. When the mandated new European Database on Medical Devices becomes available in 2022,
it is hoped that it will enhance overall transparency and provide better access to the information
that the companies already provide to the regulatory authorities.38
RECOMMENDATIONS
To help combat the sale of unsafe and ineffective products, online sellers should be required to
oversee the safety and compliance with applicable laws of the products they are selling in your
country. In addition, databases should be improved so that everyone can access an accurate, up-to-
date, and easily searchable database of products that the appropriate agencies have determined to be
safe. Rather than a variety of industry-supported databases, we suggest that the regulatory authorities
create an easily searchable database of “approved or cleared” medical products, using the device
UDI.33 This number should appear both on the product and in its advertising. The appropriate
authorities already have all the information required for such a database, and some databases do
